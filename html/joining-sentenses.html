Joining Sentences: Mastering Verbal Ability

Joining sentences, also known as sentence combining or sentence synthesis, is a common question type in verbal ability tests. These questions assess your understanding of grammar, sentence structure, and how to express ideas concisely and effectively. The goal is to combine two or more short sentences into one longer, grammatically correct sentence *without* losing or changing the original meaning.

Understanding the Task

You'll typically be presented with two or three short, related sentences. Your task is to select the option that best combines these sentences into a single, coherent sentence. The correct option will:

  • Maintain the original meaning.
  • Be grammatically correct.
  • Be concise and clear.
  • Flow logically.

Tips and Tricks

  • Identify the Relationship: Before trying to combine the sentences, understand the relationship between them. Are they showing cause and effect? Contrasting ideas? Adding information? Understanding the connection will help you choose the right conjunctions and sentence structure.
  • Look for Keywords: Pay attention to keywords that signal relationships, such as:
    • Cause and Effect: *because, since, as, therefore, consequently*
    • Contrast: *although, though, even though, but, yet, while, whereas*
    • Addition: *and, moreover, furthermore, in addition, besides*
    • Time: *when, while, as, before, after, until, since*
    • Place: *where, wherever*
  • Use Appropriate Conjunctions: Conjunctions are words that connect words, phrases, or clauses. Choosing the right conjunction is crucial for creating a grammatically correct and meaningful combined sentence.
  • Simplify and Combine Phrases: Look for phrases that can be combined or simplified. For example, if two sentences have the same subject, you can often combine the verbs into a single phrase.
  • Avoid Redundancy: Don't repeat information unnecessarily. If a word or phrase appears in multiple sentences, see if you can use it only once in the combined sentence.
  • Check for Correct Punctuation: Pay attention to punctuation, especially commas, semicolons, and dashes, when combining sentences.
  • Practice, Practice, Practice: The more you practice combining sentences, the better you'll become at it. Work through examples and pay attention to the different ways sentences can be combined.

Example with Detailed Explanation

Original Sentences:

  1. The dog barked loudly.
  2. The dog wagged its tail excitedly.

Options:

  1. The dog barked loudly, and it wagged its tail excitedly.
  2. The dog barked loudly and wagged its tail excitedly.
  3. The dog, barking loudly, wagged its tail excitedly.
  4. The dog barked loudly; however, it wagged its tail excitedly.

Explanation and Solution:

  1. Option A: This is grammatically correct but slightly redundant. "It" is unnecessary since we already know the subject is "the dog."
  2. Option B: This is the best option. It's concise, grammatically correct, and clearly shows that both actions (barking and wagging) are happening simultaneously. It correctly uses the conjunction "and" to join the two verb phrases.
  3. Option C: This is also grammatically correct, using a participial phrase ("barking loudly"). It implies that the barking is the primary action while the tail-wagging occurs.
  4. Option D: This option uses the conjunction "however," which implies a contrast. There's no contrast between barking and wagging excitedly; these actions are likely happening together.

Therefore, Option B is the most effective and accurate combination of the original sentences.
